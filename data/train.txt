Sentiment analysis, also called opinion mining or emotion AI, uses natural language processing and text analysis to identify and study emotional states and subjective information. It's widely used in customer reviews, social media, and healthcare to support marketing, customer service, and clinical applications. The basic task classifies text polarity as positive, negative, or neutral at document, sentence, or feature levels. Advanced approaches examine specific emotional states like anger, disgust, or surprise. This involves distinguishing factual statements from opinion-based ones. An objective sentence presents facts, while a subjective one expresses personal judgment or predictions. This distinction helps improve overall classification performance. This examines sentiments expressed about specific attributes. For example, a hotel review might praise location but criticize food quality. This captures nuances that general polarity analysis misses. Beyond identifying sentiment, this measures the degree of emotional expression, distinguishing between good and excellent, for instance. Three main approaches exist: knowledge-based techniques using predefined emotion words, statistical machine learning methods, and hybrid approaches combining both. Deep learning models have improved analysis of implicit sentiment in news articles and other complex domains. Sentiment analysis struggles with negations, sarcasm, and cultural nuances. Human raters typically agree only about eighty percent of the time, setting a practical ceiling for automated systems. Computer errors differ from human ones, machines often misinterpret jokes or wordplay that humans handle easily. Beyond marketing and customer service, sentiment analysis supports political forecasting via analysis, healthcare monitoring for adverse drug reactions, and recommendation systems that leverage user review sentiment. Human verification remains important, as automated systems alone cannot reliably capture historical context or platform-specific patterns.
Natural language processing is the computational handling of human language by computers. As a subfield of computer science intertwined with artificial intelligence, it encompasses speech recognition, text classification, language understanding, and text generation. It emerged in the nineteen fifties, building on foundational work in computing and intelligence. The field experienced two major eras: symbolic processing from the fifties through early nineties, and statistical processing from the nineties onward. Early symbolic approaches used hand-coded rules and knowledge bases. Automatic translation was demonstrated, though progress proved slower than anticipated. Systems demonstrated language processing capabilities despite limited vocabularies and knowledge bases. The eighties represented the peak of rule-based methods, focusing on parsing, grammar, and semantic analysis. However, the late eighties brought a fundamental shift toward machine learning algorithms, driven by increased computational capacity. Statistical and neural network methods now dominate the field. These approaches excel at handling common cases, managing unfamiliar inputs, and scaling effectively. Neural network-based methods have increasingly replaced traditional statistical approaches through semantic networks and word embeddings. Machine learning proves particularly valuable for low-resource languages and preprocessing tasks, while symbolic methods retain utility in specific applications requiring limited training data. Major processing activities include morphological analysis, syntactic parsing, semantic interpretation, and discourse understanding. Applications range from machine translation and sentiment analysis to question-answering systems and dialogue management. Recent trends emphasize increasingly abstract cognitive aspects, multilingual processing, and elimination of symbolic representations in favor of end-to-end neural systems.
Machine learning is a field within artificial intelligence focused on developing statistical algorithms that can learn from data and generalize to new situations without explicit programming. The field has grown significantly since its inception. Earlier foundational work included research on neural structures and the development of learning machines. By the nineties, machine learning emerged as its own distinct field, shifting away from symbolic approaches inherited from artificial intelligence toward methods based on statistics and probability theory. Machine learning systems aim to generalize from experience, performing accurately on new, unseen examples after training on labeled datasets. The field balances model complexity with performance; overly simple models underfit data while excessively complex ones overfit, reducing generalization ability. The field traditionally divides into three categories. Supervised learning uses labeled training data to learn mappings between inputs and outputs. Unsupervised learning discovers patterns in unlabeled data through clustering and dimensionality reduction. Reinforcement learning trains systems to maximize cumulative rewards through interaction with environments. Semi-supervised approaches combine small amounts of labeled data with larger unlabeled datasets, often improving accuracy significantly. Machine learning overlaps substantially with statistics but differs in principal goals: statistics makes population inferences while machine learning identifies predictive patterns. The field also connects to data mining, optimization, and data compression techniques.
